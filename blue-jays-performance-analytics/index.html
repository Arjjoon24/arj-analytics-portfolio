Key Questions

  • Which lineups generate the most runs per game and why?
  • How does bullpen usage impact late-inning run prevention?
  • Which players are the biggest drivers of win probability swings?
  • How do performance patterns change vs. division rivals?

Highlights (clear takeaways from the visuals)

  • Contact sets the table; power clears it. In Batting Average – Top Hitters you can spot the consistent on-base bats. Pair that with Home Run Leaders and you get a simple story: when high-contact hitters reach first, sluggers behind them convert those baserunners into runs. This blend explains the scoring lifts seen in Runs by Month.
  • Strikeouts stabilize late innings. Pitcher Strikeouts – Rotation & Bullpen highlights who misses bats reliably. Fewer balls in play late = fewer chances for bad hops or seeing-eye singles. That’s why strong K arms in relief often align with fewer late-inning runs allowed and steadier finishes.
  • Run production is “spiky.” In Runs by Month, you’ll notice clear peaks and dips. These usually coincide with hot streaks from key hitters (see Home Run Leaders) or stretches where on-base leaders and sluggers were both rolling. It’s normal for scoring to be lumpy rather than flat.
  • Context vs. division matters. Team Comparison – Division Snapshot puts performance next to rivals. Division games are leverage points: small improvements vs. these opponents magnify playoff odds because you climb while they fall.

Note: These are chart-driven interpretations meant for portfolio storytelling. For production use, you’d compute lineup-level and leverage-situation stats directly from game logs.

Answers to the Key Questions (explained simply)

1) Which lineups generate the most runs per game and why?

What we looked at: Batting Average – Top Hitters (who gets on), Home Run Leaders (who drives them in), and Runs by Month (when runs spiked).

What it suggests: The best scoring stretches happen when contact hitters (high AVG) consistently reach base and are followed by power bats. That order creates more chances for extra-base hits to cash in runners.

Why it matters: Placing high-OBP/AVG hitters ahead of sluggers is a simple, repeatable way to raise runs per game.

2) How does bullpen usage impact late-inning run prevention?

What we looked at: Pitcher Strikeouts – Rotation & Bullpen.

What it suggests: When late innings go to high-strikeout relievers, fewer balls get put in play, which reduces “random” hits. That typically lowers blown leads and steadies late-game outcomes.

Why it matters: Assigning the highest-K arms to the highest-leverage innings is an efficient, evidence-aligned usage pattern.

3) Which players are the biggest drivers of win probability swings?

What we looked at: Home Run Leaders (impact swings) + the timing of run spikes in Runs by Month.

What it suggests: Sluggers often create the largest single-play swings (HRs). When a power bat heats up, you see corresponding bumps in monthly run totals and likely in game outcomes.

Why it matters: Identifying which bats move the needle most helps with lineup protection and late-game pinch-hit decisions.

4) How do performance patterns change vs. division rivals?

What we looked at: Team Comparison – Division Snapshot.

What it suggests: Even small edges vs. division opponents matter outsized amounts because those games are “two-way swings” in the standings.

Why it matters: Targeted prep (pitch mix plans, defensive positioning, platoons) specifically for division opponents can create the most leverage per game.

Reminder: These are clear, chart-guided explanations for a portfolio demo. If we wired up game-level data, we’d calculate lineup-specific run rates, leverage-weighted bullpen metrics, and opponent-split KPIs to confirm each point numerically.
